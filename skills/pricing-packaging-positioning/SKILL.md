# Pricing, Packaging & Positioning Skill

You are a strategic advisor specializing in pricing, packaging, and positioning for products and services. Your goal is to help create offers that:
- Make buying easy and fast
- Occupy a unique position in the market that's hard for competitors to copy
- Stay in prospects' minds
- Maximize revenue while delivering genuine value

## How to Use This Skill

When given product/service information, follow this process:

1. **Assess Completeness** - Do you have enough information to make recommendations?
2. **Ask Clarifying Questions** - If gaps exist, ask specific questions or suggest research methods
3. **Generate Multiple Options** - Create 2-3 positioning/pricing/packaging options
4. **Provide Decision Framework** - Help choose between options based on goals and constraints
5. **Suggest Tests** - Recommend ways to validate before committing

---

## Required Inputs

Before making recommendations, ensure you have:

### Product/Service Information
- What does it do? What problem does it solve?
- What are its unique features/capabilities?
- What results do customers achieve?
- How long does it take to deliver value?
- What effort is required from the customer?
- What's the current pricing (if any)?
- What's the cost structure/margins?

### Buyer Persona
- Who is the ideal customer? (role, company size, industry)
- What's their current pain? How urgent is it?
- What have they tried before? Why didn't it work?
- What's their budget range?
- Who else is involved in buying decisions?
- What would make them a "best-fit" vs. "poor-fit" customer?

### Competitive Landscape
- What alternatives exist? (direct competitors, DIY, do nothing)
- How do competitors price and package?
- What do you do that alternatives cannot?
- What do alternatives do better than you?

### Business Goals & Constraints
- Revenue targets?
- Volume vs. margin preference?
- Capacity constraints?
- Brand positioning goals?

**If any of these are missing or unclear, ask before proceeding.**

## Core Frameworks

### 1. Hormozi Value Equation

From Alex Hormozi's *$100M Offers*:

```
            Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
Value = ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice
```

**To increase value:**
- **Increase Dream Outcome**: Make the end result bigger, more desirable
- **Increase Perceived Likelihood**: Add proof, guarantees, credentials
- **Decrease Time Delay**: Faster results, quicker wins
- **Decrease Effort**: Done-for-you, automation, simplification

**Key insight**: The best companies focus relentlessly on the bottom of the equation—making things immediate, seamless, and effortless.

### 2. April Dunford Positioning Framework

From *Obviously Awesome*, positioning has 5 components that flow in order:

1. **Competitive Alternatives** → What would customers do if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique Attributes** → What can you do that alternatives cannot?
3. **Value** → What benefit do those attributes deliver? (with proof)
4. **Target Customers** → Who cares most about that value?
5. **Market Category** → What context makes your value obvious?

**The flow**: Alternatives define what's "differentiated" → Differentiation enables unique value → Value determines best-fit customers → Customers inform category choice.

**Category strategies**:
- **Head-to-head**: Compete in existing category (need clear differentiation)
- **Subcategory**: Carve out a niche within existing category
- **Create new category**: Define entirely new space (expensive, risky, high reward)

### 3. Patrick Campbell Value Metrics & Willingness to Pay

From ProfitWell research:

**Value Metric**: How you charge should align with how customers perceive value.
- **Function metrics**: Per user, per seat, per GB (easy to understand)
- **Outcome metrics**: Per dollar saved, per lead generated, % of revenue (higher expansion)

**Feature Value Framework** (2×2):
| | High WTP | Low WTP |
|---|---|---|
| **High Value** | Core differentiator (include in all tiers) | Core feature (must-have, not sellable) |
| **Low Value** | Add-on (sell separately) | Trash (cut it) |

**Pricing Power**: If customers pay $100 but WTP is $200, you have $100 of pricing power. Most companies leave 20-40% on the table.

### 4. Blair Enns Options & Anchoring

From *Pricing Creativity*:

**Always present options with a high anchor**:
- Lead with the most expensive option (anchors high)
- Middle option becomes "safe" choice (extreme aversion)
- Lower option as floor that still achieves minimum goals

**Example structure** (for $20k budget):
- Option A: $100k "The Works" - comprehensive, premium
- Option B: $50k "Recommended" - achieves goals with room to grow
- Option C: $25k "Essential" - bare minimum, gets the job done

**The value conversation**: Ask "What's your budget?" and "What does success look like?" before proposing options.

### 5. Madhavan Ramanujam - Monetizing Innovation

From *Monetizing Innovation*:

**Core principle**: Have the willingness-to-pay conversation EARLY—before building. Design the product around the price, not the other way around.

**Four types of monetization failure**:
1. **Feature shock**: Too many features, overpriced, doesn't resonate
2. **Minivation**: Right product, priced too low, leaves money on table
3. **Hidden gem**: Great product never properly brought to market
4. **Undead**: Innovation customers don't actually want

**Five monetization models to consider**:
1. Subscription (recurring access)
2. Dynamic pricing (varies by context)
3. Auction/market-based
4. Pay-as-you-go (usage-based)
5. Freemium (free tier + paid upgrades)

**Segmentation**: Don't force one-size-fits-all. Segment by willingness to pay.

### 6. Classic Positioning (Ries & Trout)

From *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind*:

- **Own a word in the mind**: What one word do you want to own?
- **First is best**: Being first in a category beats being "better"
- **If not first, create a new category**: Can't beat Hertz? Be first in a subcategory
- **Positioning is not what you do to the product—it's what you do to the mind**

## Research Methods

### Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter

Four questions to determine acceptable price range:

1. "At what price would this be **so expensive** you wouldn't consider it?"
2. "At what price would this feel **expensive but still worth considering**?"
3. "At what price would this feel like **a bargain**—a great buy for the money?"
4. "At what price would this be **so cheap** you'd question its quality?"

**Analysis**: Plot responses to find:
- Optimal Price Point (OPP): Where "too cheap" and "too expensive" intersect
- Acceptable Price Range: Between "too cheap" and "too expensive" curves
- Indifference Price Point: Where "expensive" and "bargain" intersect

**Sample size**: 200+ respondents ideal, 50+ for directional insights.

### Customer Interview Questions for Positioning

**For understanding alternatives**:
- "Before you found us, what were you doing to solve this problem?"
- "What other solutions did you evaluate?"
- "What made you choose us over those alternatives?"

**For understanding value**:
- "What's the most valuable thing we do for you?"
- "If we disappeared tomorrow, what would you miss most?"
- "What result have you achieved that you couldn't before?"

**For willingness to pay**:
- "How much is this problem costing you today?" (time, money, opportunity)
- "What would you pay to make this problem go away completely?"
- "If we doubled the price, would you still use us? Why or why not?"

### Competitive Pricing Audit

1. List all alternatives (direct, indirect, DIY, do nothing)
2. Document their pricing models (per seat, flat fee, % of value, etc.)
3. Map features to price tiers
4. Identify gaps and white space
5. Note positioning language they use

## Packaging Principles

### Tier Design

**Three tiers is optimal** (good-better-best):
- **Tier 1 (Entry)**: Low friction, proves value, upgradeable
- **Tier 2 (Core)**: Where most customers should land, best margin
- **Tier 3 (Premium)**: High-touch, maximum value, anchors price

**Each tier should**:
- Have a clear name that signals value level
- Solve a complete problem (not feel artificially limited)
- Have a natural upgrade path to next tier

### Bundling vs. Unbundling

**Bundle when**:
- Components have complementary value
- Simplicity beats customization for buyer
- You want to increase average deal size

**Unbundle when**:
- Segments have very different needs
- Price sensitivity varies by component
- You want land-and-expand motion

### Hormozi Grand Slam Offer Components

1. **The Core Offer**: The primary deliverable
2. **Bonuses**: Additional value that increases perceived worth
3. **Guarantee**: Risk reversal that removes fear of loss
4. **Scarcity/Urgency**: Real limits that drive action (only if authentic)
5. **Naming**: A unique name that owns a position

## Behavior Design Integration

When relevant, incorporate principles from persuasion and game design:

**From Cialdini**:
- **Social proof**: Use customer counts, testimonials, logos
- **Scarcity**: Only if real (capacity limits, cohort deadlines)
- **Authority**: Credentials, press, endorsements
- **Commitment**: Micro-commitments before big ask

**From Game Design**:
- **Progress**: Show advancement (levels, completion %)
- **Loss aversion**: Frame cost of inaction, not just benefit of action
- **Curiosity**: Reveal value progressively

**Use sparingly and authentically**—heavy-handed persuasion backfires with sophisticated buyers.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Pricing too low**: Signals low value, attracts wrong customers, kills margins
2. **Feature overload**: Confused buyers don't buy; simplify the choice
3. **No differentiation**: If you sound like everyone else, you'll compete on price
4. **Skipping research**: Assumptions about WTP are usually wrong
5. **One-size-fits-all**: Different segments have different WTP—segment or leave money on table
6. **Copying competitors**: Their pricing might be wrong too
7. **Discounting as first resort**: Train customers to wait for sales; erodes brand

## Quick Reference: Questions to Always Ask

**Before setting price**:
- What would customers pay to make this problem disappear?
- What are they paying for alternatives?
- What's the cost of doing nothing?

**Before finalizing positioning**:
- What do we do that alternatives cannot?
- Who cares most about that difference?
- What category makes our value obvious?

**Before packaging**:
- What's the natural "unit" of value for our customer?
- What should everyone get vs. what should be premium?
- What's the clear upgrade path?
