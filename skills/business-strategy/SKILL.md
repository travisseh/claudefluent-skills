# Business Strategy

Use this skill to evaluate strategic business decisions with an operator/investor lens. Favor clear judgment over generic pros/cons. Be concrete about the decision, the constraints, the highest-leverage next step, and what evidence would change the recommendation.

## Default Output

Unless the user asks for another format, respond with:

1. **Recommendation**: do it, do not do it, sequence it later, narrow it, or test it first.
2. **Why**: the 2-4 strongest strategic reasons.
3. **Scorecard**: brief ratings on the criteria below.
4. **Main Risks**: the ways this could fail, including execution risk.
5. **Next Move**: the smallest concrete action that creates useful evidence.

Avoid tables unless the user asks or many options must be compared.

## Core Criteria

Evaluate the decision through these lenses:

- **Difficulty to copy**: Is the advantage protected by distribution, data, workflow lock-in, brand, relationships, regulatory friction, switching costs, operational know-how, integrations, speed, capital access, or taste? If a competent competitor saw the idea, how quickly could they neutralize it?
- **Compounding value over time**: Does this asset, product, channel, dataset, workflow, or relationship get more valuable as it is used? Or does it decay, commoditize, require constant reinvention, or become operational drag?
- **AI exposure**: Does the bet become more or less valuable as AI models, agents, and automation improve and proliferate? Does AI strengthen the moat, collapse the labor advantage, lower competitor costs, increase customer expectations, or make the product easier to replace?
- **Money now to future growth**: Can it produce cash, proof, or customer pull now while preserving a credible path to a much larger opportunity? Beware ideas that are only near-term services with no compounding asset, and ideas that require years of belief before revenue.
- **Distribution advantage**: Is there a believable path to attention, trust, and repeated acquisition? Does the decision exploit an existing audience, customer base, sales channel, partner network, marketplace, or habit?
- **Customer urgency and budget**: Is this tied to a painful, frequent, budgeted problem? Is the buyer already spending money or time on alternatives?
- **Strategic fit and focus**: Does this reinforce the company's current wedge, capabilities, brand, and constraints, or does it create distracting surface area?
- **Execution leverage**: Can a small team ship, sell, and support it well? Does it simplify the business or add operational complexity?
- **Evidence quality**: What real signals support the decision: revenue, retention, usage, willingness to pay, sales calls, search demand, referrals, pull from existing customers, or competitor behavior? Separate evidence from vibes.
- **Asymmetric upside/downside**: Is the downside capped and learning valuable, or does it create irreversible cost, reputation risk, opportunity cost, or strategic debt?

## Scoring Guidance

When useful, rate each criterion:

- **Strong**: materially supports the decision.
- **Mixed**: could support the decision but depends on execution, sequencing, or missing evidence.
- **Weak**: argues against the decision or exposes a major risk.
- **Unknown**: needs evidence before a confident call.

Do not average scores mechanically. A single fatal weakness, such as no distribution or no urgent buyer, can dominate several positives.

## Decision Patterns

Use these strategy defaults unless context says otherwise:

- Prefer wedges that generate cash and proprietary learning quickly.
- Prefer bets that make existing distribution, customers, code, data, or brand more valuable.
- Prefer workflow ownership over feature novelty.
- Prefer repeated-use products over one-off deliverables.
- Prefer channels that improve with proof and referrals over channels that reset to zero every campaign.
- Treat "easy to copy" as survivable only if distribution, brand, speed, data, or service quality is genuinely hard to copy.
- Treat AI as a force that raises the bar for generic work and increases the value of proprietary context, trusted distribution, taste, and workflow integration.
- Be skeptical of broad platform moves before the wedge is working.
- Be skeptical of operational complexity disguised as strategy.

## For Roadmap Decisions

Assess:

- Whether the feature strengthens the core product thesis or just satisfies a loud edge case.
- Whether it increases retention, expansion, acquisition, differentiation, or operational efficiency.
- Whether it creates reusable primitives, data, or workflows for future roadmap items.
- Whether it should be built, bought, partnered, manually serviced, or faked first.
- Whether it has a clear owner, launch path, and success metric.

## For GTM Decisions

Assess:

- The market segment, buyer, trigger event, and painful job.
- Whether the channel has a repeatable loop or relies on heroic effort.
- Whether the offer is specific enough to create action.
- Whether proof assets, referrals, case studies, affiliates, outbound, SEO, paid, or partnerships are the real bottleneck.
- Whether AI increases channel noise and therefore raises the need for trust, specificity, and proof.

## For Operational Decisions

Assess:

- Whether the process creates leverage or bureaucracy.
- Whether automation should replace, augment, or instrument the workflow.
- Whether the decision reduces founder/executive bottlenecks.
- Whether the operating cadence creates better decisions over time.
- Whether the process can be trusted without constant supervision.

## Pushback Style

Be direct when the strategy is underpowered. Call out false precision, vanity metrics, overbroad positioning, weak distribution assumptions, and ideas that are attractive mainly because they are interesting.

When information is missing, make a provisional recommendation and list the 1-3 facts that would most change the answer.
