# Pre-Mortem: Nan + ClaudeFluent deal
**Date:** 2026-05-09 | **Event date:** Near-term negotiation | **Stakes:** Quitting ExampleCo, $240k-$320k income replacement, ClaudeFluent ownership, career path, family runway

## The ways this fails

### 1. The deal solves cash anxiety but creates ownership regret
- **Likelihood:** Medium-High
- **Impact:** Very High
- **Early warning signs:** You accept 22% potential equity because it feels "earned, not purchased," without defining exactly what durable asset Nan contributes beyond help, momentum, and execution.
- **Leading indicator:** You cannot state in one sentence what Nan uniquely owns that makes ClaudeFluent worth materially more in 12 months.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Define equity around durable value creation: retained revenue, repeatable channel, operating system, team trainings, partner network, or instructor/sales machine. Do not vest equity merely for temporary revenue spikes.

### 2. GP split definitions become a fight later
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** "COGS" is left vague, affiliate commissions are verbally assumed, and nobody writes down how paid ads, instructors, tools, refunds, discounts, payment fees, or founder comp are treated.
- **Leading indicator:** No written definition of revenue, COGS, GP, incremental revenue, attribution, refund handling, and payment timing.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Write a one-page economics definition before negotiating percentages. COGS should include direct attributable costs: ads, affiliate/referral commissions, payment processing, contracted instructors/support, and required delivery tools. Exclude general overhead and founder comp unless explicitly agreed.

### 3. You give equity for revenue you would have gotten anyway
- **Likelihood:** Medium-High
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** Revenue from your existing LinkedIn audience, past students, referrals, and warm network counts toward Nan's equity milestones even when his contribution is unclear.
- **Leading indicator:** No attribution rule for "Nan-created" vs "organic/the user-created" revenue.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Use milestone credit only for mutually defined growth channels or net-new revenue sources Nan materially helped create. At minimum, exclude existing pipeline and already-scheduled classes.

### 4. The hourly bridge turns into a second job
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** $120/hr x 16 hrs/week becomes 16 billable hours plus Slack, calls, strategy noodling, and context-switching.
- **Leading indicator:** ClaudeFluent gets fewer than two protected deep-work blocks per week after the Nan work starts.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Put the consulting work into fixed blocks with a hard cap. Unused hours do not roll into nights/weekends. Async questions wait until the next block unless there is a true emergency.

### 5. The class model cannot reach escape velocity
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** You optimize for replacing $240k-$320k income instead of building toward $1M-$2M of value/cashflow in 12-24 months. The business keeps selling $800 seats in 5-person classes, which works for proof but does not create enough leverage.
- **Leading indicator:** End-to-end class burden exceeds 8-10 hours per class including sales, prep, delivery, follow-up, and support.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Define the escape-velocity model explicitly: $1M-$2M in 12-24 months likely requires higher AOV, team trainings, corporate packages, affiliates, repeatable cohorts, instructor leverage, or a productized implementation layer. The solo class model should be a proof engine, not the company.

### 6. The milestones reward gross activity, not enterprise value
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** Equity unlocks on "revenue above baseline" instead of retained gross profit, repeatable acquisition, customer outcomes, or durable operating assets.
- **Leading indicator:** A one-time launch or affiliate push could trigger equity without creating repeatability.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Tie milestones to trailing 3-month revenue/GP, retained customers, paid team engagements, or channel repeatability. Use a trailing average, not a single-month spike.

### 7. You quit ExampleCo but do not convert the freed attention into escape velocity
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** The first 30 days after quitting fill with curriculum polish, reactive student help, Nan collaboration, admin, and scattered experiments instead of a focused growth sprint.
- **Leading indicator:** No weekly scoreboard for revenue, qualified leads, partner asks, team-training pitches, affiliates recruited, and delivery leverage shipped.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Treat quitting ExampleCo as buying one thing: concentrated founder attention. Before resigning, define the 90-day sprint plan and the weekly metrics that prove ClaudeFluent is moving toward $1M-$2M potential.

## Worst case: the story of how this goes wrong

You take the deal because it feels like a smart middle path: Nan protects some downside, ClaudeFluent gets more oxygen, and the equity only unlocks if growth happens. But the contract stays fuzzy. Some revenue comes from your existing network, some comes from affiliates, some from Nan's help, and attribution turns into a monthly argument. Meanwhile, you are teaching 8-10 classes/month, doing Nan work two days/week, and still carrying the founder burden. Twelve months later, Nan owns a meaningful piece of ClaudeFluent, the business is still mostly the user-shaped, and you resent having sold part of the upside before proving whether you needed to.

## Golden path: if this works perfectly

- **Perfect outcome:** Nan's hourly bridge buys a clean 90-day runway while the equity deal converts him into a true growth/operator partner only if he creates durable, attributable revenue and systems. ClaudeFluent reaches salary-replacement cashflow quickly, but does not stop there: it builds toward $1M-$2M of value/cashflow within 12-24 months through team trainings, referrals, affiliates, higher-ticket offers, and delivery leverage.
- **Backward chain:** The economics were defined cleanly; equity vested only on durable net-new growth; Nan's 16 hrs/week stayed capped; ClaudeFluent shifted from individual classes to higher-ticket packages; you built instructor/sales leverage before volume forced it.
- **Decisive move:** Separate the deal into two instruments: a 90-day paid consulting bridge and a separate equity earn-in with attribution, trailing metrics, vesting, and founder protections.
- **Non-obvious constraint:** The scarce asset is not time. It is clean attribution plus delivery leverage. Without those, more revenue creates conflict and burnout.
- **What to stop doing:** Stop using salary replacement as the main decision frame. It is a minimum safety threshold, not the goal. The real question is whether this deal helps ClaudeFluent reach escape velocity faster than you could alone.

- Require equity milestones to vest on trailing 3-month GP or retained revenue, not one-time revenue.

- Push for a sunset/cap on the 30% GP split or make it apply only to Nan-sourced channels.
- Use the next 90 days to sell $15k-$25k team trainings so $30k/month does not require 8 separate classes.

## Questions before I forecast the win

1. Is the 30% GP share meant to apply to all ClaudeFluent revenue, all incremental revenue, or only Nan-sourced/new-channel revenue?
2. Does Nan's hourly payment continue if he earns equity, or is the hourly bridge temporary?
3. Do the equity milestones measure monthly revenue, annualized revenue, cumulative incremental revenue, or trailing-period revenue?
4. What happens if revenue hits the milestone and then churns/drops?
5. Who owns curriculum, brand, student list, website, content, and any AI/course tooling if the partnership ends?
6. What explicit decisions would Nan control at 5%, 15%, or 22% ownership?

## Best case: the story of how this works

The win came because you did not treat Nan's proposal as one blob. You took the cash bridge, protected your calendar, and negotiated equity as an earn-in for durable growth rather than vibes. Nan built or unlocked a real acquisition channel, affiliates and team trainings became repeatable, and the class model stopped depending on you personally filling every seat. The first inflection point was a $20k team training that sold from a warm intro and created two referrals. By the end of the year, ClaudeFluent was big enough that your decision was not "can I survive without ExampleCo?" but "what is the highest-leverage company this wants to become?"

## Next bottlenecks (10x test)

**The key input here is: paid ClaudeFluent demand.**
**If that 10x'd tomorrow, here's what breaks first:**

### Bottleneck 1: Delivery capacity
- **Why it breaks:** At $800/student and 5 students/class, $80k/month requires 20 full classes/month unless pricing, packaging, or team deals change.
- **Systematize now:** Build the class runbook, async onboarding, instructor notes, assistant checklist, reusable exercises, and post-class support templates.

### Bottleneck 2: Attribution and partner economics
- **Why it breaks:** More channels mean more disputes: affiliate, Nan, your LinkedIn, past students, organic search, referrals. Without rules, growth creates conflict.
- **Systematize now:** Use source tracking, CRM attribution, written channel definitions, and a monthly economics close process.

### Bottleneck 3: Sales-call capacity
- **Why it breaks:** Demand creates qualification calls, objections, follow-up, scheduling, payment friction, and custom requests. You are currently the closer and delivery lead.
- **Systematize now:** Create a qualification form, 2-call close process, objection doc, pricing menu, and "not a fit" criteria.

## Gut check: would you still do this?

Yes, but renegotiate scope/price/timing around escape velocity, not income replacement. The hourly bridge is reasonable if it is clean and capped. The 70/30 GP split with you at 70% can be reasonable if COGS and attribution are defined. The 22% equity earn-in is the part to slow down on: it should require durable, attributable, retained value creation that helps ClaudeFluent become a $1M-$2M opportunity within 12-24 months, not just revenue crossing a line. The right version of this deal accelerates a company. The wrong version makes you feel safer while capping the upside too early.
