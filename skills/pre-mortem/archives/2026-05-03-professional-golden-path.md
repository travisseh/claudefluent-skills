# Pre-Mortem: Professional golden path after ExampleCo RIF
**Date:** 2026-05-03 | **Event date:** May-June 2026 negotiation and allocation window | **Stakes:** $240k salary, ClaudeFluent upside, ExampleCo equity, family runway, ReviewCo recovery, reputation, and personal bandwidth

## The ways this fails

### 1. You accept a ExampleCo equity bump that is still structurally too small
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** They frame 1% as generous without giving you current cap table, liquidation preferences, option strike, vesting, acceleration, board-approved grant terms, or what "success" would actually mean in dollars.
- **Leading indicator:** You do not receive a written offer with fully diluted ownership, strike price, vesting, refresh mechanics, and exit math under realistic scenarios.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Treat "1%" as a discussion opener, not the deal. Ask for a real package: 2-4% fully diluted or a performance-based path there, salary protection, role/title clarity, and either half-day/week protected ClaudeFluent time or a 4-day comp structure. Require exit math before emotionally valuing it.

### 2. ClaudeFluent misses the timing window because ExampleCo consumes the recovery narrative
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** LinkedIn stays paused, corporate-training lessons remain in your head, affiliate follow-ups drift, Nan waits for direction, and "I just need to get through this ExampleCo week" repeats for 4-6 weeks.
- **Leading indicator:** Fewer than 3 public posts and fewer than 5 partner/affiliate/customer follow-ups per week.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Install a non-negotiable ClaudeFluent operating cadence: 2 LinkedIn posts/week, 1 sales/partner block, 1 product/curriculum block, and 1 delivery-system block. If ExampleCo wants you to carry more load, the protected time is part of the price.

### 3. You confuse corporate-training excitement with a scalable business model
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** Every company engagement gets custom-built from scratch, you do prep calls yourself, sessions vary wildly, and only one of three sessions creates "rainbows" excitement.
- **Leading indicator:** No reusable corporate training package exists within 14 days: promise, audience, preflight checklist, exercises, pricing, follow-up CTA, and testimonial system.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Productize corporate training into one flagship offer before selling more. Make it modular: executive demo, IC workshop, team implementation sprint, and follow-up office hours. Do not sell bespoke AI transformation theater.

### 4. You over-partner and lose the tight feedback loop that makes ClaudeFluent good
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** Nan/Kaiyo discussions drift from "drive revenue and earn equity" into broad cofounder language before roles, milestones, decision rights, and exit terms are clear.
- **Leading indicator:** Any partner gets equity without revenue milestones, vesting, clawback, ownership boundaries, and explicit decision domains.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Keep the partnership narrow and milestone-based. Revenue-driven equity only. No vague strategic equity. Define who owns acquisition, sales ops, creative, delivery, curriculum, finance, and brand direction.

### 5. The brand gets trapped in "Claude" while demand shifts to Codex
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** Medium-High
- **Early warning signs:** Prospects start asking "Do you teach Codex?" while the site, cohort, and posts imply Claude-only expertise.
- **Leading indicator:** More than 20% of inbound conversations mention Codex, GPT-5.5, OpenAI, or "AI coding tools" rather than Claude Code specifically.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Keep ClaudeFluent as the company for now, but introduce an "AI Coding Fluency" umbrella in copy and launch a Codex track or workshop as an extension, not a rebrand. Rebrand only if market proof forces it.

### 6. Financial pressure makes you too conservative and too scattered at the same time
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Impact:** Medium-High
- **Early warning signs:** You avoid quitting because cash risk is real, but also do not negotiate a hard ExampleCo boundary, so ClaudeFluent gets scraps. Meanwhile taxes, subscriptions, and Provo claim sit open and generate background dread.
- **Leading indicator:** No explicit runway number and no weekly life-admin block by May 10.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Separate risk reduction from upside creation. Keep salary for now, but buy protected time. Spend one fixed weekly 90-minute block on taxes, subscriptions, and Provo claim until the obvious leaks are closed.

### 7. Bishopric/family load silently caps your available founder energy
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** Medium
- **Early warning signs:** ClaudeFluent work only happens late at night, Steph gets less predictable support, bishopric tasks are guilt-driven instead of system-driven, and sleep quality degrades.
- **Leading indicator:** You cannot name your weekly protected blocks for family, church, ExampleCo, and ClaudeFluent.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Build the plan around energy reality, not theoretical hours. ClaudeFluent needs 6-10 protected high-quality hours/week, not 15 guilty fragmented hours.

## Worst case: the story of how this goes wrong

ExampleCo offered 1%, you accepted because it felt like the responsible move, and the company used your ambition to get executive-level output at employee-level upside. ClaudeFluent stayed alive but stopped compounding: LinkedIn remained inconsistent, Nan never got a clean operating lane, affiliates cooled off, and competitors filled the category while you were stabilizing someone else's company. By August, taxes and the Provo claim were still unresolved, Steph/family pressure was higher, and the ExampleCo equity still looked theoretical. You had avoided one obvious risk but let the highest-upside asset lose momentum.

## If this works: the next year

### 1 month from now
- **What's true:** You have a written ExampleCo offer with real equity math, not vibes. ClaudeFluent posting has restarted, the corporate-training blueprint is documented, and Nan/Kaiyo has a milestone-based growth agreement draft. You have a Codex-oriented offer sketched without panic-rebranding.
- **Where your time goes:** 4.5 days/week ExampleCo, 0.5 protected day plus 2 focused blocks for ClaudeFluent, one life-admin block.
- **What's getting hard:** The calendar is tight, and ExampleCo may test whether the half-day is real or just nominal.

### 3 months from now
- **What's true:** ClaudeFluent has a repeatable weekly acquisition cadence, 2-4 active affiliates, and at least one productized corporate training package sold or in serious pipeline. Revenue is lumpy but no longer feels like pure personal-network extraction.
- **Where your time goes:** LinkedIn/content, partner management, sales calls, curriculum/productization, and flagship delivery. ExampleCo remains demanding but bounded.
- **What's getting hard:** Delivery quality and sales follow-up both want your personal attention. You need either an operator, delivery assistant, or sales/partner owner.

### 6 months from now
- **What's true:** ClaudeFluent is either clearly on a path to replacing salary or clearly not. A credible range is $15k-40k/month depending on corporate training conversion and affiliate growth. You have enough data to decide whether to leave ExampleCo, reduce further, or stay while compounding CF.
- **Where your time goes:** Less raw teaching prep, more offer design, partner leverage, sales standards, and customer outcomes.
- **What's getting hard:** Brand architecture. If Codex/GPT demand dominates, ClaudeFluent either becomes "ClaudeFluent by history, AI coding fluency by market" or you create a broader parent brand.

### 1 year from now
- **What's true:** Best realistic case: ClaudeFluent is a $40k-100k/month training and enablement business with cohorts, corporate training, community, and affiliates/partners. You have one or two high-leverage collaborators and the option to leave ExampleCo without lighting the family's financial safety on fire.
- **Where your time goes:** Closing flagship relationships, setting curriculum/product standards, managing partner channels, and preserving the personal voice that made the business work.
- **What's getting hard:** The business needs operations. Scheduling, billing, cohort success, testimonials, affiliate tracking, corporate follow-up, and curriculum freshness cannot all live in your head.

## Best case: the story of how this works

You did not quit ExampleCo impulsively, but you also did not let ExampleCo rent your entire ambition for 1%. You used the RIF moment to negotiate a real executive package and protected ClaudeFluent time, then restarted LinkedIn and converted the corporate-training lessons into a packaged offer. Nan/Kaiyo became a performance-tied acquisition arm instead of a fuzzy cofounder conversation, and affiliates turned into a real channel. By late 2026, ClaudeFluent had enough repeatable revenue that leaving ExampleCo became a controlled decision instead of a leap. The key inflection point was treating time as the scarce asset in the ExampleCo negotiation, not just salary or equity.

## Next bottlenecks (10x test)

**The key input here is: ClaudeFluent demand.**
**If that 10x'd tomorrow, here's what breaks first:**

### Bottleneck 1: the user as the only delivery and sales engine
- **Why it breaks:** At 10x leads, you cannot post, sell, customize, teach, follow up, collect testimonials, and improve curriculum while keeping ExampleCo, family, and bishopric intact.
- **Systematize now:** Write the ClaudeFluent operating manual: offer menu, sales script, corporate discovery questions, delivery checklist, post-class follow-up, testimonial prompt, and affiliate onboarding.

### Bottleneck 2: Corporate-training customization
- **Why it breaks:** Every company thinks its workflows are special. If you accept that premise, each training becomes a bespoke consulting project and destroys margins.
- **Systematize now:** Create three reusable modules: "AI coding foundations," "agentic workflow for real codebases," and "team rollout/guardrails." Allow only 20% customization.

### Bottleneck 3: Partner and affiliate governance
- **Why it breaks:** More partners create more ambiguity: who owns lead source, commission, equity credit, customer success, and renewals?
- **Systematize now:** Create a simple partner ledger and rules of engagement before scaling: source attribution, payout timing, refund treatment, revenue milestone definitions, and equity vesting terms.
