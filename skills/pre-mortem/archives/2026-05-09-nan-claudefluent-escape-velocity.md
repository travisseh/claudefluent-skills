# Pre-Mortem: Nan + ClaudeFluent escape-velocity deal
**Date:** 2026-05-09 | **Event date:** Near-term negotiation | **Stakes:** Quitting ExampleCo, ClaudeFluent ownership, $1M-$2M outcome in 12-24 months, family financial runway

## The ways this fails

### 1. Nan is useful but not company-making
- **Likelihood:** Medium-High
- **Impact:** Very High
- **Early warning signs:** Nan improves ideas, adds momentum, helps with execution, and makes the leap feel safer, but does not unlock a repeatable channel, enterprise buyer access, operator leverage, or a materially higher AOV model.
- **Leading indicator:** After 30 days, you cannot point to one new scalable mechanism Nan created: channel, offer, sales motion, operator system, or team-training pipeline.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Make the equity thesis explicit: "Nan earns ownership only if he materially increases the odds ClaudeFluent becomes a $1M-$2M asset/cashflow engine faster than I could alone." Convert that into milestones tied to durable growth, not helpfulness.

### 2. The deal optimizes for downside protection instead of upside acceleration
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** The conversation centers on replacing your $240k income, reducing fear, and buying two days/week instead of building the machine that can create $1M-$2M within 12-24 months.
- **Leading indicator:** The operating plan has income-replacement math but no 90-day escape-velocity scoreboard.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Treat salary replacement as the floor, not the goal. Define the 90-day test around leading indicators: team-training pipeline, affiliate/referral channel, premium offer validation, instructor leverage, and weekly revenue growth.

### 3. You sell equity for revenue that is not durable
- **Likelihood:** Medium-High
- **Impact:** Very High
- **Early warning signs:** Equity unlocks on "revenue above baseline" even if the revenue comes from a launch spike, existing network, affiliate blast, or one-time cohort push.
- **Leading indicator:** Milestones use gross or cumulative revenue without retention, trailing-period, attribution, or channel-quality requirements.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Tie equity to trailing 3-month gross profit, retained revenue, repeatable channels, team-training contracts, or operating systems that survive without Nan personally pushing every deal.

### 4. The class model becomes a comfortable trap
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** ClaudeFluent can reach $30k-$45k/month with enough $800 seats, so it feels like the leap worked, but the path to $100k+/month still requires too much of you.
- **Leading indicator:** More than 60% of revenue depends on you personally selling and teaching small classes after 90 days.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Force a second revenue motion immediately: $15k-$50k team trainings, premium cohorts, corporate implementation packages, affiliate distribution, or instructor-led cohorts.

### 5. GP split definitions create attribution conflict
- **Likelihood:** High
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** "Revenue," "COGS," "GP," "affiliate costs," "Nan-sourced," and "incremental" stay conversational instead of contractual.
- **Leading indicator:** No written definition of COGS, including affiliate/referral commissions, paid ads, payment fees, instructors, refunds, discounts, delivery tools, and founder compensation exclusions.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Define economics before percentages. COGS should include direct attributable costs to acquire, sell, and deliver revenue: ads, affiliate/referral commissions, processing fees, contractors/instructors, and required delivery tools. General overhead and founder comp should be excluded unless explicitly agreed.

### 6. Quitting ExampleCo buys time but not focus
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** The first 30 days after quitting are filled with curriculum polish, scattered experiments, student support, Nan strategy, and admin instead of distribution and leverage.
- **Leading indicator:** No weekly scoreboard for revenue, qualified leads, team-training pitches, partner asks, affiliates recruited, close rate, and delivery hours reduced.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Before resigning, write the 90-day post-ExampleCo sprint. Every week should produce pipeline, sales assets, delivery leverage, or proof that a larger channel can work.

### 7. Governance gets ignored because the relationship feels high-trust
- **Likelihood:** Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Early warning signs:** You discuss vision, upside, and collaboration, but not control rights, IP, distributions, sale decisions, dilution, deadlock, future ventures, or what happens if one person loses interest.
- **Leading indicator:** No explicit answer to what 5%, 15%, or 22% ownership allows Nan to approve, block, access, or claim.
- **Mitigation (do this now):** Keep control, IP, brand, curriculum, and future-venture rights clean. Equity can have economics without broad control. Get attorney review before signing anything.

## Worst case: the story of how this goes wrong

You quit ExampleCo because Nan's bridge makes the leap feel financially rational. ClaudeFluent gets more time and revenue grows, but mostly through the same small-class, founder-led model. Nan helps, but the help is hard to separate from your existing momentum and network. Equity starts vesting because revenue crosses thresholds, not because a scalable machine exists. A year later, ClaudeFluent is maybe a good income stream, but not a $1M-$2M opportunity, and you have already sold a meaningful chunk of the upside to someone who did not fundamentally change the trajectory.

## Golden path: if this works perfectly

- **Perfect outcome:** The Nan deal helps ClaudeFluent reach escape velocity: $1M-$2M value/cashflow potential within 12-24 months, with salary replacement achieved as a byproduct. Nan earns economics because he creates durable acceleration: a channel, operating system, higher-ticket sales motion, or leverage layer you would not have built as quickly alone.
- **Backward chain:** You quit ExampleCo into a pre-defined 90-day sprint; Nan's role was tied to concrete growth mechanisms; the class model was repositioned as proof and cashflow, not the final company; team trainings or premium B2B offers became the main expansion path; equity vested only on retained, attributable, repeatable growth.
- **Decisive move:** Reframe the negotiation from "protect my downside" to "prove you increase ClaudeFluent's odds of becoming a $1M-$2M business faster than I can alone."
- **Non-obvious constraint:** The constraint is not courage to quit. It is designing a business model where growth does not require the user personally doing every sale, class, and support interaction.
- **What to stop doing:** Stop treating revenue milestones as sufficient proof of partner value. A partner should earn equity for changing the slope, not merely participating while the line rises.

## Next bottlenecks (10x test)

**The key input here is: qualified demand for ClaudeFluent.**
**If that 10x'd tomorrow, here's what breaks first:**

### Bottleneck 1: Founder-led delivery
- **Why it breaks:** At $800/student and 5 students/class, scaling by classes alone forces you into 10-20+ classes/month. That caps the company at your calendar.
- **Systematize now:** Build instructor notes, async onboarding, assistant roles, reusable exercises, QA standards, and a delivery runbook.

### Bottleneck 2: High-ticket sales motion
- **Why it breaks:** Individual classes can sell from trust and content. $15k-$50k team trainings need discovery, procurement, customization boundaries, proposals, and follow-up.
- **Systematize now:** Create a team-training offer page, proposal template, discovery script, case-study template, pricing menu, and follow-up sequence.

### Bottleneck 3: Attribution/economics
- **Why it breaks:** More channels create more claims on the same dollar: affiliates, Nan, organic, alumni, LinkedIn, partnerships. Growth without source truth becomes conflict.
- **Systematize now:** Use CRM/source tracking, channel definitions, monthly economics close, and written rules for which revenue counts toward Nan milestones.
