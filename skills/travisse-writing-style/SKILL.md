# the user Writing Style Guide

Use this guide when drafting messages, emails, or any written communication on behalf of the user.

## ABSOLUTE RULE: ZERO EM-DASHES, EVER

This is the single most-violated rule. Read it twice.

**NEVER use the em-dash character `—` (Unicode U+2014). Not in emails. Not in Slack. Not in iMessage. Not in LinkedIn posts. Not in articles. Not in blog posts. Not anywhere.**

This applies whether the em-dash is:
- Spaced (` — `)
- Unspaced (`—`)
- Double-hyphen (`--`)
- Triple-hyphen (`---`)
- Auto-converted by an editor

**Replacement options (in order of preference):**
1. Restructure the sentence so no dash is needed
2. Use a comma, period, or colon
3. Use a regular hyphen with spaces (` - `) only if no other punctuation works

**Before showing any draft to the user, scan it for `—` characters. If you find ANY, rewrite that sentence. Do not ship a draft with em-dashes in it. Drafts with em-dashes get rejected on sight.**

## Voice & Tone

- **Warm but direct** - Friendly and approachable, but gets to the point
- **Professional yet casual** - Adapts formality to context (more formal for church leadership, casual for family/friends)
- **Polite and considerate** - Uses softening language, acknowledges others' time
- **Optimistic and encouraging** - Positive framing, grateful tone

## Openers

- **"Hey"** is the default opener for most messages (15% of messages start with "Hey")
- Use recipient's name after "Hey" for more personal touch: "Hey Steph", "Hey guys", "Hey man"
- For group messages or announcements: "Hey guys" or "Dear [Group Name]"
- Quick responses often skip the opener entirely

## Common Phrases

the user frequently uses:
- "Would you be up for..." / "Would you be willing to..."
- "Just" as a softener ("Just checking in", "Just wanted to...")
- "Let me know if..."
- "Thanks for..." / "Thank you for..."
- "Sorry for the slow response"
- "Hope your [time period] is/was going well"
- "Sounds good"
- "No worries" / "Zero worries"
- "Great!" / "Awesome!" / "Perfect!"

## Punctuation & Style

- **Exclamation points**: Used moderately (22% of messages) - shows enthusiasm without overdoing it
- **Question marks**: Used appropriately (28% of messages)
- **Periods**: Sometimes omitted at end of casual messages
- **Capitalization**: Generally proper, but quick replies may start lowercase
- **Emojis**: Very minimal (1% of messages) - occasional 🙏 or ❤️ but not a heavy emoji user

## Message Length

- Average message: ~100 characters
- Varies widely based on context:
  - Quick acknowledgments: "Yes", "K", "Sounds good"
  - Detailed requests/instructions: Multiple sentences, sometimes broken into separate messages
- Long messages often broken into multiple texts rather than one wall of text

## Context-Specific Patterns

### Church/Bishopric Communications
- More formal opener: "Hey [Name], this is the user Hansen from the bishopric"
- Clear ask with options: "Would you be willing to speak the third or fourth Sunday?"
- Grateful closing: "Thanks for being willing!"
- Follows up with helpful details

### Work Communications
- Direct and efficient
- Uses links and concrete next steps
- Professional but not stiff

### Family/Personal
- Very casual
- Short messages
- More likely to use "K" instead of "Ok"
- Practical/logistical focus

## Things to Avoid

- **Em-dashes (`—`, `--`, `---`)** - NEVER use em-dashes in any form. See the ABSOLUTE RULE at the top of this file. Restructure the sentence, use a comma/period/colon, or use a regular hyphen with spaces (` - `) as a last resort.
- Heavy emoji use
- Overly formal language ("Dear Sir/Madam", "Please be advised")
- Long unbroken paragraphs
- Passive aggressive tone
- Being too wordy when direct is better

## Apologetic Patterns

the user apologizes when appropriate but doesn't over-apologize:
- "Sorry for the slow response"
- "Sorry about that"
- "Sorry to go back and forth"

## Sample Messages

**Asking someone to speak in church:**
> Hey Steven and Erica been thinking about you guys - would you be up for speaking in sacrament meeting the third or fourth Sunday in January?

**Quick acknowledgment:**
> Sounds good man

**Following up:**
> Hey just checking in if you've had any feelings on calling presidency members or want to do some visits

**Showing appreciation:**
> Beautiful thank you!!!

**Rescheduling:**
> Hey I'm running 15 late let's do 1:45!

# LinkedIn & Long-Form (ClaudeFluent Content Charter)

**MANDATORY:** Before drafting ANY LinkedIn post, blog post, or article on the user's behalf, follow this section. The casual voice rules above still apply (no em-dashes, warm-direct tone, no guru voice), but long-form content has additional rules.

## Step 1: Ask Which Mode

Before writing, always ask the user which mode he wants (unless he's already specified):

> Which mode should I write this in?
> 1. **Super Timely AI News** — fresh AI release, model drop, or industry shift translated for non-engineer operators. What happened, why it matters to you specifically, and what to do about it now. Post within 24-48 hours of the news or don't bother.
> 2. **Tested Guides, Tutorials & Recommendations** — something the user has actually used, built, or taught. The proof is in the testing: "I ran this with X people, here's what worked." Drives readers to a specific guide, tool, or workflow.
> 3. **Founder Journey** — what's actually happening building ClaudeFluent (and adjacent ventures): wins, losses, decisions, surprises, things he's learning the hard way. First person, peer-to-peer, no triumphalism.
> 4. **Opinion / Thought Leadership** — an opinionated frame, lens, or take on how AI is reshaping work, careers, software, or the industry. Stands on the strength of the take itself, not a fresh test or news event. Gives readers a vocabulary or map they didn't have. Has to risk being wrong.

Wait for his answer before drafting. If he picks one, tag the draft with the mode at the top so he knows which frame you used.

## The One Reader

Every post is written to ONE person, never to a category:

> A mid-career operator (PM, founder, head of growth, solo builder) smart enough to see the whole system but stuck in a role that forces them to specialize. Feels dumb about the parts outside their lane. Suspects AI is making generalists valuable again. Wants proof they can hold the whole map in their head without being a genius.

NEVER write "to product people," "to founders," "to marketers" as a category. Write to that one person.

## Core Frame: Filling Out the Map

the user's professional identity is end-to-end legibility on closed systems — ads top-to-bottom, monorepos, cold prospecting, Claude Code workflows. The fire comes from seeing the whole territory at once. ClaudeFluent is the product expression of this. Content should be on-brand for this frame.

## Mode Playbooks

### Mode 1 — Super Timely AI News
- Fresh AI release, model drop, product update, or industry shift translated for the one reader who isn't an engineer.
- Structure: what happened (1-2 sentences), why it matters to you specifically (the bulk), and what to do about it right now.
- the user's angle is always "I tested this with real people" or "here's what this means for your workflow." Never repackage a press release.
- Link to a ClaudeFluent guide or resource when one exists. This is also the distribution mode for new content.
- Keep it grounded. No hype, no "this changes everything." Just: here's the thing, here's what it does, here's whether you should care.
- **Timeliness is the whole point.** Post within 24-48 hours of the news or don't bother. If it's not actually timely, it's the wrong mode.

### Mode 2 — Tested Guides, Tutorials & Recommendations
- Something the user has actually run with real people, built end-to-end, or taught in class. No theoretical advice.
- The proof is in the testing: "I ran this workflow with X PMs, here's what worked and what didn't." Or: "I built this skill, used it for 3 weeks, here's the version I'd actually keep."
- Lead with the result or the recommendation, then back-fill with the test that earned the right to say it.
- Drives the reader to a specific destination: a ClaudeFluent guide, a tool, a workflow, a setup. Always include the link.
- This is where the deep ClaudeFluent content lives. Article launches, new guides, new skills, new tutorials.
- If you can't say "I tested this," it's a Mode 1 (news) or Mode 3 (founder journey) post. Reject the recommendation framing.

### Mode 3 — Founder Journey
- What's actually happening building ClaudeFluent (and adjacent ventures: ExampleCo, ReviewCo, the rental property, side experiments).
- Real updates: wins, losses, decisions, surprises, lessons learned the hard way, numbers when relevant.
- Peer-to-peer voice. the user talking to other operators/founders, not pitching to prospects.
- No triumphalism, no humblebrag. If the post would feel weird at a dinner with friends, kill it.
- Specific is better than general. "We hit X this month and the unexpected lesson was Y" beats "growing a startup is hard."
- This is also the mode for half-formed thinking, public bets, "I'm trying X for the next month, here's what I'm watching for."

### Mode 4 — Opinion / Thought Leadership
- An opinionated frame, lens, or take on how AI is reshaping work, careers, software, knowledge labor, or the industry.
- Not tied to fresh news (Mode 1), a specific test/recommendation (Mode 2), or a personal update (Mode 3). The post stands on the strength of the take itself.
- The take must be defensible: grounded in what the user has actually seen across hundreds of operators, students, customers, or his own building, not generic prediction.
- Gives the reader a vocabulary or a map they didn't have. Names the tiers, the categories, the trade-offs. "AI Consumer / Worker / Builder" beats "AI is changing work."
- Has to risk being wrong. If everyone in the comments would nod along, it's not a take, it's wallpaper.
- Lead with the claim or the frame, not throat-clearing. No "I've been thinking about…" intros.
- Supporting evidence (e.g. "I've taught this to a few hundred operators and the pattern is clear") is fine, but it's backing for the take, not the point of the post.
- Drives to ClaudeFluent, a guide, an article, or a follow-on piece when natural, but the destination is secondary to the frame.
- This is the right mode for: market frames, contrarian calls, "the wrong way to think about X", career-architecture takes, and reframes of conventional wisdom.

## Ban List (applies to ALL long-form content)

Never:
- Humblebrags ("so humbled to share…")
- Fake vulnerability (performative small-failure + platitude lesson)
- Generic tips ("5 ways to…", "what nobody tells you about…")
- "Here's what I learned" posts with no actual specific learning
- LinkedIn guru voice, hooks that feel engineered
- Em-dashes in any form (`—`, `--`, `---`). See ABSOLUTE RULE at top of file. Restructure or use comma/period/colon. Last resort: spaced hyphen ` - `.
- Softening real opinions to make them palatable
- Writing to a category ("product people") instead of the one reader
- Mixing modes in a single post
- **Staccato AI-slop cadence**: strings of three or more consecutive short declarative sentences stacked back-to-back ("The pitch is simple. You define the tasks. Anthropic runs it. All on their side."). Reads as machine-generated every time. Vary sentence length - mix short punchy lines with longer ones connected by commas, "and", "which", "because", or "so". A single short sentence for emphasis after a longer one is great. Three short sentences in a row is slop. If you just wrote three short declaratives back-to-back, merge at least two of them into one longer clause.

## Drafting Checklist (run before showing the user any draft)

1. Which mode is this? (Tagged at top of draft.)
2. Is it written to the one reader, not a category?
3. Passes the ban list?
4. Mode 1 (Super Timely AI News): is the news genuinely fresh (last 24-48 hours) and is the angle "what to do about it now"? (Stale or angle-less = wrong mode.)
5. Mode 2 (Tested Guides): can you point to the specific test that earned the right to recommend? Is there a clear destination link? (No real test = wrong mode.)
6. Mode 3 (Founder Journey): is it a real update with specifics, not a generic "startups are hard" reflection? Would it feel weird at dinner with friends? (Triumphalism / humblebrag = rewrite.)
7. Mode 4 (Opinion / Thought Leadership): is there a clear, defensible take that risks being wrong? Does it give the reader a frame or vocabulary they didn't have? (No risk + nod-along = wallpaper, not a take.)
8. Does the cringe feel "a little scary" (good) or "slightly fake" (bad)?
9. **Zero em-dashes?** Literally search the draft for `—` before showing it. If you find any, rewrite. This is the #1 violation that gets drafts rejected.
10. Sentence-length variance check: scan every paragraph for three+ consecutive short sentences. If you find any, merge them into flowing prose with commas and conjunctions.

## Format Notes (LinkedIn-specific)

- Short paragraphs, often single sentences. LinkedIn rewards whitespace.
- Open with a concrete hook — a moment, a number, a claim. Not a question.
- No hashtag spam. One or two max, or zero.
- Don't end with "What do you think?" unless the question is genuine and specific.
- Length: favor medium (150-300 words) over short tweets or long essays, but follow what the content demands.

## Format Notes (Blog/Article-specific)

- Lead with the thesis or the moment. No throat-clearing intros.
- Subheads that are claims, not categories ("The map-filling framework fails when X" not "Background").
- First person throughout. the user writes from his chair, not from above.
- End with the specific takeaway, not a generic call to action.
